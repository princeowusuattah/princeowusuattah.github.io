MediTrak Vital Signs Dashboard

Overview

MediTrak Life is a telemedicine startup developing proprietory continuous tracking technology and digital therapeutics to help health care practitioners conduct precision medicine. MediTrak is supported by the Purdue Foundry and Elevate Ventures. I worked as a UX Consultant leading the design of its vital sign analytics dashboard.

Challenge

Problem Space

MediTrak was developing a vital signs monitor with its own GUI. Recognizing the value of data insights, they decided to expand their feature on a desktop-enabled platform. Since that they were already in the development stage, they needed to build a modular platform that allowed future intergration of other feature.





Solution

A cloud-based a single-page web application that allows physicians and senior home facilities to monitor vital sign data by through trend analysis, host patients electronic medical records and ultimately offer healthcare remotely.




User Login

The single page login system allows both the physicians and patient's facility to access the application from the same page but branch into different parts of the software



Patient Overview

The patient overview has the live patient dashboard that shows their vital signs. It also shows functionalities for communicating and connecting with different medical types of equipment.



Trends Dashboard

The trends feature help both service provides the opportunities to analyses health trends over some time by navigating the visualizations of different health data.

Virtual Appointments

The facility where the patient is being cared for can schedule virtual regular and emergency visits with available physicals or a patient's devoted doctor.

Outcome

The software is currently waiting emergency use authorization and has led MediTrak to raise $150,000 in seed funding



Research

Research Methods

User Interviews

I kicked off the project by gaining a thorough understanding of the problem space in order to frame an actionable problem path. I started by sitting in on a meeting with the CEO, who also served as be the product manager, and two research scientists who were collaborators of the project.

Stakeholder meetings

I also interviewed two doctors and a senior living nurse, who served as project resources.Each interview lasted from one to two hours. I was interested in gaining a better view of the problem space and existing solutions.These interviews were more like an introduction than a straightforward investigation.





Research Insights

1. Preventing avoidable hospitalization

The broader issue meditrak was attempting to solve was preventing unnecessary hospitalization by collecting actionable data so physicians can make time-sensitive decisions without any hospitalization."

27 million individuals with private insurance attend the emergency department, two-thirds of which are "avoidable" and "not a true emergency.



2. Modular solution a requirement

The physical band was in its early stage, so any supporting digital product should a modular as much as possible. I was introduced to several existing and analogous telehealth solutions through this interview.



3. Addressable the core user group

At the MVP level, we established the core two core users group for the potential solution; Facility — Personnel from the facility in which a person uses the systems. E.g., Senior home nurse and CNA’s Physicians — Doctors who use the medical data from the monitor suggest prescribing care for the users.

The core user group happens to be physcians and the vital sign user's facility



Thematic Analysis

Interview Insights

After the interviews were transcibed,I used the Job-to-be-Done Framework to thematically analysed the key insights in other to group the themes into user requirements. I narrowed it down to the most impactful feature, that would require minimum effort from the development team.

User Modelling

I generated insights into users that represented the two major audiences targeted by this early version to communicate design problems and direction to the team. While personas have numerous pitfalls, they helped me clearly define key issues.


Design Directtion

Problem Statement

With all the data I had gathered through the interviews and user modeling as synthesis, I was able to frame a people's problem that was realistic and actionable.


"Senior homes want to minimize preventable hospitalization but don't an end-to-end platform to delivery health services remotely."



We narrowed down design direction and focused on the four core design drection for the early version.




Design

Design Methods

Task Analysis

Having gathered all the information I needed, I went on to develop the initial flows. This was the stage most of the ideation was done on the specific designs. This was done iteratively since I was getting feedback and critiques from all the team members. The major improvements I made were the name and titling of key features.


Single-page application onboarding

The onboarding flow was streamlined to have a similar flow. The flow was simplified for ease and learnability. Beyond this, it was easier to build the two diverging flows since they could be duplicated with little effort.



Facility Dashboard Flow

Based on the requirements generated from our initial research, our core users for the first version for the software was the facility and physicians. The facility flow has all the core features but also have the ability to add physicians



Physicians Dashboard Flow

The physician flow which is similar to the facility flow focuses on the general service delivery side. The flow also indicates the facility details and can also confirm insurance details.
